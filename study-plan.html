Exam Format — What You Are Preparing For

Written Exam — 2 hours, one long question. Always a Closed Structure analysis split into 5 parts. Total weight ≈ 30+ points.

PartWhat you must doPoints
(a)Verify isostatic — compute DSI = ED + ID1
(b)Global equilibrium → external support reactions3–4
(c)Draw the Initial Free-Body Diagram (split into sub-systems, label unknowns)4
(d)Draw the Final FBD (solve sub-system equilibrium, find all internal hinge forces)4–6
(e)Draw N, V, M diagrams (functions per segment + critical values)15–18

Oral Exam — 45 minutes, three problems.

#What to expect
P1Concept — Method of nodes vs Method of sections (Ritter): purpose & differences
P2DSI for 3 structures → isostatic / hypostatic / hyperstatic; for unstable cases, draw the failure mechanism
P3N, V, M diagrams — usually a three-hinged arch or simple portal frame (smaller than the written)

Knowledge Map — What You Need to Learn

Six topic clusters. The stars indicate exam weight: more stars = more points on the actual exam.

  • A. Basics foundation — no direct exam points, but needed for everything

    • A1. Units & vector algebra
    • A2. Force composition, couples, moments
    • A3. Varignon's principle, statically equivalent forces
    • A4. Support types & degrees of freedom (pin / roller / fixed / double pendulum)

  • B. Equilibrium & Reactions needed for written (b), oral P3 prep

    • B1. Rigid body equilibrium: Sum Fx=0, Sum Fy=0, Sum M=0
    • B2. Support reactions
    • B3. Distributed loads (uniform / triangular / trapezoidal)

  • C. Degree of Static Indeterminacy (DSI) ★★★ oral P2 + written (a)

    • C1. DSI = ED + ID
    • C2. ED = external constraints − 3
    • C3. ID = internal constraints − 3 per cut
    • C4. Classify as isostatic / hyperstatic / hypostatic
    • C5. Draw the failure mechanism for unstable cases

  • D. Trusses ★★★ oral P1

    • D1. Method of joints (node equilibrium)
    • D2. Method of Ritter (sections — cut through 3 bars)
    • D3. Purpose / differences — the oral P1 answer

  • E. Internal action diagrams N / V / M ★★★ written (e) + oral P3 — biggest weight

    • E1. Section method; N, V, M sign conventions
    • E2. 27 master problems on simple beams (a)–(aa)
    • E3. Differential relations: dV/dx = −q, dM/dx = V (M extremum where V = 0)
    • E4. Portal frames N / V / M
    • E5. Three-hinged arch — high frequency in oral P3

  • F. Closed structures ★★★★★ integrates A–E; this IS the written exam

    • F1. ED + ID for closed loops
    • F2. Split into 3 sub-systems
    • F3. Initial FBD: label all sub-system unknowns
    • F4. Final FBD: 9 equations to solve every internal force
    • F5. Per-bar N / V / M with the rule of compass consistency check

How to Self-Study

Reading the slides

  • Spend no more than 3 minutes per slide. If something is unclear, mark it and keep going.
  • After each slide deck, close it and write 3 summary sentences in your own words. If you cannot, re-read it.
  • Save unclear points and bring 3 or more to your tutor in one batch.

Solving exercises

  • Try independently — do not peek at the answer.
  • Compare with the solution PDF in the 04_Solved_Exercises folder.
  • Mark mistakes with X and write one line: where I went wrong.
  • Once you have 3+ errors, schedule the next tutor Q&A.

5 Golden Rules — Pin These Above Your Desk

  1. When stuck, mark and move on. Never spend 30 minutes on one slide.
  2. Always draw the Free-Body Diagram first. No FBD = no points.
  3. Counter-clockwise moment is always positive. Use this convention consistently.
  4. At every bend in a frame, M must be continuous. Use this as a free error-check.
  5. Your error log matters more than your notes. Track every mistake; you will see patterns.

Good luck. Statics rewards consistency, not last-minute effort. If you keep the error log and finish the master problems honestly, you are already in good shape.
