Problem 4.3 — Roof truss, Ritter's method Problem 4.4 — Square truss, joints + Ritter
Sign convention (used everywhere below): every unknown bar force is drawn assuming tension — the cut bar pulls the free body toward the far node. A positive result ⇒ tension (+); a negative result ⇒ compression (−). You never need to guess the sense in advance.

Problem 4.3 — Roof truss · all bar forces by Ritter's method

Given structure

Step 0 — Is it isostatic?  +  geometry

DSI = ED + ID = (3 reactions − 3 DOFs) + 0 = 0 → isostatic ✓ Truss check: m + r = 13 + 3 = 16 = 2n = 2·8 ✓ Rafter angle (every inclined bar has the same α): tan α = 1.25 / 2.50 = 0.5 α = 26.57°, sin α = 0.4472, cos α = 0.8944

Step 1 — Reactions

Σ Fx = 0: HA = 0 Σ MA = 0 (CCW⁺): −10·(2.5) + 7.5·(5) − 10·(7.5) + VB·(10) = 0 ⇒ VB = 62.5 / 10 = 6.25 kN ↑ Σ Fy = 0: VA + 6.25 − 10 − 10 + 7.5 = 0 ⇒ VA = 6.25 kN ↑
Loads (10 + 10 − 7.5 = 12.5 kN) and structure are both symmetric about the vertical axis H–D ⇒ VA = VB = 12.5/2 = 6.25 kN. We only need to solve the left half; the right half mirrors it.

Selection of cuts

The rule for choosing a cut

Each cut may intersect bars with at most 3 unknown internal forces (3 equilibrium equations per free body). Knowns from earlier cuts don't count.

CutBars intersectedUnknownsGives
1AF, FC, CD3NAF, NFC, NCD
2FH, FD, CD (known)2NFH, NFD
3FH (known), HD, HG2NHG, NHD
4AF (known), AC1NAC
Any other sequence of valid cuts works too — this is the one used in the official solved PDF, so you can compare line by line. Everything on the right half follows by symmetry.
Cut 1 — free body {A, C} · unknowns NAF, NFC, NCD
reaction (known) unknown bar force (assumed tension)

3 unknowns ⇒ 3 equations

Σ MF = 0 (CCW⁺): ← F kills NAF & NFC NCD·(1.25) − 6.25·(2.5) = 0 ⇒ NCD = +12.5 kN Σ Fx = 0: NCD + NAF·cos α = 0 ⇒ NAF = −12.5 / 0.8944 = −13.98 kN Σ Fy = 0: 6.25 + NAF·sin α + NFC = 0 ⇒ NFC = −6.25 − (−13.98)(0.4472) = 0 kN
NCD = 12.5 kN (tension)  ·  NAF = 13.98 kN (compression)  ·  NFC = 0
Shortcut you could have used: joint C connects only AC–CD (collinear) + CF (perpendicular stub), with no load at C ⇒ zero-force member rule: NCF = 0 by inspection. Same rule gives NEG = 0 on the right half.
Cut 2 — free body {A, C, F} · unknowns NFH, NFD (NCD already known)
reaction known bar force unknown (assumed tension)

2 unknowns ⇒ 2 equations

Σ Fx = 0: 12.5 + NFH·cos α + NFD·cos α = 0 ⇒ NFH + NFD = −12.5/0.8944 = −13.98 (1) Σ Fy = 0: 6.25 − 10 + NFH·sin α − NFD·sin α = 0 ⇒ NFH − NFD = 3.75/0.4472 = +8.39 (2) (1)+(2): 2·NFH = −5.59 ⇒ NFH = −2.80 kN (1)−(2): 2·NFD = −22.36 ⇒ NFD = −11.18 kN
NFH = 2.80 kN (compression)  ·  NFD = 11.18 kN (compression)
Do not forget the external 10 kN at F — it is inside this free body. Forgetting applied loads on the kept part is the #1 Ritter error.
Cut 3 — free body {H} · unknowns NHG, NHD (NFH known)
known bar force unknown (assumed tension)

2 unknowns ⇒ 2 equations

Σ Fx = 0: 2.80·cos α + NHG·cos α = 0 ⇒ NHG = −2.80 kN (= NFH, as symmetry predicts) Σ Fy = 0 (↑⁺): 2.80·sin α − NHG·sin α + 7.5 − NHD = 0 1.25 + 1.25 + 7.5 − NHD = 0 ⇒ NHD = +10.0 kN
NHG = 2.80 kN (compression)  ·  NHD = 10.0 kN (tension)
⚠ The official handwritten PDF has a typo here. It concludes “NHD = 7.5 kN” and carries +7.5 into the final drawing — but plug the numbers into its own equation: 2.79·sin 26.56° + 2.79·sin 26.56° + 7.5 = 1.25 + 1.25 + 7.5 = 10.0. The printed answer sheet (Practical_notes_4.pdf, p. 6) correctly shows 10.00. The trap: at joint H it is tempting to say “the bar under the load just carries the 7.5 kN” — wrong, because the two rafters also push up at H (2×1.25 kN), and HD must pull the node down against all of it. Cross-check with joint D: ΣFy at D: NHD = −(NFD + NDG)·sin α = −(−22.36)(0.4472) = +10.0 ✓
Cut 4 — free body {A} · unknown NAC (NAF known)

1 unknown ⇒ 1 equation is enough

Σ Fx = 0: NAC − 13.98·cos α = 0 ⇒ NAC = 13.98 · 0.8944 = +12.5 kN (check Σ Fy: 6.25 − 13.98·sin α = 6.25 − 6.25 = 0 ✓)
NAC = 12.5 kN (tension)
The compressed rafter pushes node A down-left; the bottom chord must pull right to hold it — that is exactly why the bottom chord of a roof truss is a tie (tension).

Symmetry → right half · final result

tension (+) compression (−) zero-force bar

Physical picture: the rafters arch the loads to the supports in compression; the bottom chord is the tie that stops the “arch” from spreading; the king post HD hangs node D from the apex. Classic king-post truss behaviour.

Problem 4.4 — Square truss · (a) method of joints, (b) Ritter's method

Given structure

Read the topology first (7 bars, 5 nodes)

The diagonal A–E–D is one straight line broken by hinge E (two bars AE + ED), plus the stub C–E. So joint E has 3 bars, two of them collinear, and no load — remember this for later.
Isostatic check: m + r = 7 + 3 = 10 = 2n = 2·5 ✓

Step 1 — Reactions (common to (a) and (b))

Σ Fx = 0: HA − 100 + 100 = 0 ⇒ HA = 0 Σ MA = 0 (CCW⁺): VB·(5) − 200·(5) − 100·(5) + 100·(5) = 0 ↑ ↑D load ↑100→ at D ↑100← at C ⇒ VB = 1000 / 5 = 200 kN ↑ Σ Fy = 0: VA + 200 − 100 − 200 = 0 ⇒ VA = 100 kN ↑
VA = 100 kN (↑)   HA = 0   VB = 200 kN (↑)
The two horizontal 100 kN forces point in opposite directions (← at C, → at D) but act at the same height — they form a couple of 100·5 = 500 kN·m that the vertical reactions must balance, together with the moment of the 200 kN load.

(a) Method of joints — solve node by node

Golden rule for the order: always move to a joint with ≤ 2 unknown bars (2 equations per joint). Here: B → A → E → C, then D is the global check. Starting at C or D directly would give 3 unknowns — stuck.
Joint B — unknowns NBA, NBD
Σ Fx = 0: −NBA = 0 ⇒ NBA = 0 Σ Fy = 0: NBD + 200 = 0 ⇒ NBD = −200 kN
NAB = 0  ·  NBD = 200 kN (compression)
B is a roller: only a vertical reaction. The 200 kN must go straight up the column DB; nothing pushes B sideways, so AB carries nothing.
Joint A — unknowns NAC, NAE (NAB = 0 known)
Σ Fx = 0: NAB + NAE·cos 45° = 0 + NAE·0.707 = 0 ⇒ NAE = 0 Σ Fy = 0: NAC + NAE·sin 45° + 100 = 0 ⇒ NAC = −100 kN
NAE = 0  ·  NAC = 100 kN (compression)
Joint E — zero-force member rule (no load, 2 collinear bars + 1 stub)
⊥ to line A–D: NCE·sin 90° = 0 ⇒ NCE = 0 ∥ to line A–D: NED = NEA = 0 (NAE = 0 from joint A)
NCE = 0  ·  NED = 0
This is the same rule you used in 4.3 at joints C and E: an unloaded joint with two collinear bars + one stub ⇒ the stub is zero, the collinear pair share one force. Worth memorizing — examiners love it.
Joint C — unknown NCD (+ consistency check on NCA)
Σ Fx = 0: NCD + NCE·cos 45° − 100 = NCD + 0 − 100 = 0 ⇒ NCD = +100 kN Σ Fy = 0 (check): −NCA − NCE·sin 45° − 100 = −(−100) − 0 − 100 = 0 ✓
NCD = 100 kN (tension)
Joint D — final verification (all bars already known)
Σ Fx = 0: −NDC − NDE·cos 45° + 100 = −100 − 0 + 100 = 0 ✓ Σ Fy = 0: −NDB − NDE·sin 45° − 200 = −(−200) − 0 − 200 = 0 ✓
Every joint closes ⇒ the solution is self-consistent. Using the last joint as a check is free insurance — always do it in the exam.
(a) Result:   NCD = +100 kN (T)  ·  NCA = −100 kN (C)  ·  NDB = −200 kN (C)  ·  NAB = NAE = NED = NCE = 0
Load-path intuition: the two opposite 100 kN horizontals balance each other through the top chord CD (tension); each vertical load drops straight down its own column (CA carries 100, DB carries 200). The whole E-web does nothing — 4 of 7 bars are zero-force.

(b) Ritter's method — bars CD, BD, ED only (forget the results of (a))

Cut selection

Step 0 — inspection before cutting

At joint E (unloaded, AE–ED collinear, CE stub): NCE = 0 and NAE = NED — allowed “for free”, it is not the method of joints solution, just the zero-force rule. This is what makes Cut Ⅱ legal (4 intersected bars, but ≤ 3 unknowns).

Why you can NOT just cut a circle around D

A section around node D intersects exactly the 3 wanted bars (CD, DB, ED) — but all three lines meet at D (concurrent forces). ΣMD becomes 0 = 0, leaving only 2 useful equations for 3 unknowns ⇒ unsolvable. A Ritter cut is only useful if the cut bars are not all concurrent. That is why we need two “proper” cuts.
Cut Ⅰ — keep the LEFT part {A, C, E} · gives NCD and NED

Pick the moment point where 2 of 3 unknowns vanish

The lines of action of NED (along A–E–D) and NAB (along A–B) both pass through A ⇒ take moments about A and only NCD survives.

Σ MA = 0 (CCW⁺): 100·(5) − NCD·(5) = 0 ← only the 100← at C has an arm ⇒ NCD = +100 kN Σ Fy = 0: 100 − 100 + NED·sin 45° = 0 ⇒ NED = 0 Σ Fx = 0 (bonus): −100 + NCD + NED·cos 45° + NAB = 0 ⇒ NAB = 0
NCD = 100 kN (tension)  ·  NED = 0
Cut Ⅱ — keep the TOP part {C, D} · gives NDB

Moments about C kill NCA and NCE

Σ MC = 0 (CCW⁺): −200·(5) − NDB·(5) = 0 ↑ 200 kN at D, arm 5 m ↑ NDB pulls down at x = 5 m (the two 100 kN horizontals pass through height of C ⇒ no arm; NED = 0 already) ⇒ NDB = −200 kN Σ Fy = 0 (bonus check): −100 − 200 − NCA − NDB = −300 − NCA + 200 = 0 ⇒ NCA = −100 kN ✓ (matches (a))
NDB = 200 kN (compression)
(b) Result (identical to (a), as it must be):   NCD = +100 kN (T)  ·  NBD = −200 kN (C)  ·  NED = 0

Joints vs Ritter — what you should take away

Method of joints (a)Method of Ritter (b)
StrategyMarch node-to-node (B→A→E→C→D), 2 equations eachOne cut straight to the target bars, 3 equations per cut
Best whenyou need all barsyou need a few specific bars
Key skillchoosing the starting joint (≤2 unknowns)choosing the cut (≤3 unknowns, not concurrent) and the moment point (where the other unknowns intersect)
Failure modestarting at a 3-unknown jointcutting 3 bars that meet at one node (like around D here)
Common mistakes in truss problems:
  1. Forgetting external loads that sit inside the kept part of a cut (the 10 kN at F in 4.3 Cut 2).
  2. Mixing sign conventions mid-problem. Assume tension everywhere; let the algebra decide.
  3. “The vertical bar under the load = the load” — false at H in 4.3 (the rafters contribute too; even the official handwritten PDF tripped here).
  4. Choosing a Ritter cut whose 3 bars are concurrent — zero information from ΣM.
  5. Not exploiting symmetry and zero-force member rules first; they cut the work in half.
