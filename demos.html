① Foundation Concepts — 用到的基础概念

Three ideas unlock both problems: moment of a force, force decomposition, and the bridge that connects them — Varignon's principle.

1️⃣ Moment of a force about a point

M = r × F = F · d
where d = perpendicular distance from the reference point to the force's line of action.
Sign convention: counter-clockwise (CCW) is positive.

r (position)
d (perpendicular dist)
F (force)

👉 Drag the green dot P anywhere.

r = (2.50, 1.50) m, |r| = varies
F = (0, 3) kN — vertical, upward
d (perpendicular) = 2.50 m
M = F · d = +7.50 kN·m
Key insight #1: drag P vertically (same x). |r| changes, but d and M don't. Moment is invariant when you slide a force along its own line of action.

Key insight #2: drag P horizontally. d changes proportionally. M = F · d.

2️⃣ Varignon's Principle

The moment of a force = sum of the moments of its components.
MF = MFx + MFy for any orthogonal decomposition.
This is the workhorse: instead of fighting a tilted force directly, decompose it and sum.

F (resultant)
Fx
Fy

👉 Drag P, slide the angle. Watch MF = MFx + MFy always hold.

r = (3.0, 2.0) m, |F| = 5 kN
Fx = 4.33 kN · Fy = 2.50 kN
MFx = −ry·Fx = -8.66
MFy = rx·Fy = +7.50
Sum = -1.16 kN·m
MF (computed directly) = -1.16 kN·m
✓ Always equals Sum — that's Varignon.
How these unlock the problems:
  • Prob 1.3: 12 forces → Varignon says Mtotal = Σ Meach. Because ∑F = 0 (pure binary), the result is independent of reference point.
  • Prob 2.3: Cable force T on the post → use Varignon backwards: decompose T into Ft (along post) + Fn (perpendicular). Solving the two components is easier than fighting T directly.
  • Trig you'll need: Law of cosines (AB² = OA² + OB² − 2·OA·OB·cos∠O) and Law of sines (sin∠A/OB = sin∠O/AB) to handle non-right triangles like in Prob 2.3.

Prob 1.3 — Pure Binary: moment is the same about any point

3 force pairs. Each pair = equal-opposite forces, so ∑F = 0. A system with ∑F = 0 is called a pure couple (or pure binary).

👉 Click or drag anywhere in the diagram to set the reference point. Watch the moment — it never changes. That's the defining property of a pure couple.

Pair 1 — 10 kN (vertical, arm 4 m)
Pair 2 — 6 kN (horizontal, arm 4 m)
Pair 3 — 4 kN (vertical, arm 2 m)
∑Fx = 0 kN
∑Fy = 0 kN
⇒ ∑F = 0 ✓ pure binary
Moment about red reference point:
M = +72.0 kN·m
(CCW ↺)
Why constant? Shifting the reference by vector r changes M by r × ∑F. Since ∑F = 0, the shift is 0. M doesn't depend on the point.

For part (c): Toggle "equivalent binary" — same M, distance d = 2 m, so F = M/d.
Note vs the actual Prob 1.3: the textbook problem has 6 binary pairs (12 forces) giving M = 252 kN·m. This demo is a simplified 3-pair version that follows the exact same logic.

Prob 2.3 — Force projection on local axes (t, n)

⚠️ Watch the angle! ∠AOB = 120°, not 60° — because B is on the opposite side of O.
T (cable tension)
Ft (along post — compression)
Fn (perpendicular)
∠AOB
Step 1 — Law of cosines:
AB² = OA² + OB² − 2·OA·OB·cos(∠AOB)
    = 5.490
AB = 2.343 m
Step 2 — Law of sines:
sin α = OB · sin(∠AOB) / AB
α = 26.33°
Ft = T · cos α = 672.24 N (compression)
Fn = T · sin α = 332.53 N
Check: √(Ft² + Fn²) = 750.0 ≈ T ✓
Try this: slide the post angle to 90° — α stays the same? slide OB to 0 — what happens to AB and α? These help build intuition for the geometry.
